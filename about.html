Atlas Research Institute

Atlas Research Institute is a free research fellowship for high school students investigating how artificial intelligence is reshaping education access around the world. The organization recruits student researchers from schools across the Global South and Global North, training them in research methodology and deploying a standardized survey instrument to build a cross-national dataset on AI, education, and knowledge access.

Atlas was founded on a specific observation: students from countries like Nepal, Bangladesh, and Bolivia consistently reported having little to no exposure to research methodology, while students from the United States, Canada, and China arrived with existing credentials and institutional support. Research literacy is a function of structural access, not student ability. Atlas exists to close that gap by giving every participating student the same tools, the same training, and the same opportunity to contribute to real academic research.

What the fellowship involves

The Atlas Research Fellowship is a six-week structured program designed for high school students with no prior research experience. Fellows are selected to represent their school, trained in survey methodology by professors at Stanford and other leading universities, and guided through the process of deploying the Atlas survey instrument at their school. The same survey is used at every school in every country, producing comparable data across contexts.

After collecting data, fellows write a policy brief that analyzes their school's results against the global dataset. These briefs can be submitted to our partner journals for publication. We also connect fellows with the policy organizations we work with so their research contributes to real education advocacy. The work goes somewhere.

What the study measures

The Atlas survey examines how students access AI tools, who introduces them to new technologies, whether families and schools support AI-assisted learning, and how trust in AI-generated knowledge varies across cultural contexts. The core research question is anthropological: how does cultural context mediate the impact of AI on education access? A student in rural Nepal using ChatGPT to learn things their school cannot teach is a fundamentally different experience than a student in suburban California whose teacher integrated AI into the curriculum. Atlas measures that difference.

What fellows receive

Published research credentials. Co-author credit on the cross-national dataset. Policy briefs submitted through partner journals for publication. Your name on real academic work before you graduate high school.

Direct access to researchers. Professors at Stanford and other leading universities work with our fellows. You learn how research actually works from people who do it for a living.

Policy connections. We work with policy organizations in education. Fellows get exposure to how advocacy works and can connect their research to people shaping education policy. Your work reaches the people making decisions.

A global research network. You're paired with a fellow in a different country to compare findings. The program is entirely free.

Who should apply

Atlas is designed for high school students in grades 9 through 12 (or international equivalents) who are interested in research, social science, education policy, AI, or global affairs. No prior research experience is required. The fellowship is open to students from any country. Atlas values geographic diversity and prioritizes building a network that represents a wide range of educational contexts.

Students looking for research opportunities for high school students, free research programs, or international student research fellowships are encouraged to join the waitlist for the Fall 2026 cohort.

How Atlas is different

Most research programs for high school students cost thousands of dollars, operate only in the United States, and produce work that nobody reads. Atlas is free, operates across multiple countries, and builds a pipeline for your work from day one. Partner journals give you a real path to publication. Policy organizations give you a real audience. The fellowship is structured so that a student in Dhaka and a student in Danville follow the same program, use the same instrument, and contribute to the same study. Equal access to research, regardless of where you live.
