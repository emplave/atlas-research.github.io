About the Institute

Who gets to learn, and who does not

A research organization built around a single question about access to knowledge.

PremiseWhy Atlas exists

Atlas started from a pattern in who arrives ready to do research. Students applying from Nepal, Bangladesh, and Bolivia routinely had no exposure to research methodology at all. Students from the United States, Canada, and China arrived with credentials and support already in place.

The difference was not ability. It was access. Research literacy tracks the resources around a student, not the student. Atlas exists to close that gap: the same training and the same standards, wherever a student happens to live.

ProgramsTwo, by design

Atlas runs two programs. The Fellowship trains students to do real research over the summer. Chapters carry that work into the school year, led by students we select ourselves.

The Atlas Fellowship

Selective, six weeks, over the summer. Fellows choose a question relating to education — from who gets access to school and how it is funded, to how students actually learn, to the effect of technology and AI on learning — and produce a policy brief or literature review backed by real evidence, whether from existing data (UNESCO, the World Bank, PISA, national education records) or their own surveys and interviews. Finished work is reviewed and published, in Atlas's own journal and in our partner journals.

Atlas Chapters

Chapters run through the school year and are led by students we invite. We select chapter heads from our fellowship applicants — exceptional students we ask to build a research community at their own school and bring others into the work. It is how the strongest Atlas work continues past the summer.

MethodWhat fellows do

The Fellowship is built for students with no prior research experience. Fellows work with professors and researchers from universities including Stanford, USC, and the University of Melbourne. They write to a fixed standard, revise against feedback, and finish with a complete piece of research.

Every finished brief goes through review before it is published. Atlas runs its own journal, the Atlas Journal of Education Policy, with a review panel and a fixed standard a brief has to meet to appear in it. Work also goes to our partner journals — the International Journal of High School Research and Curieux Review, both peer-reviewed. Atlas also partners with Lumiere Education, which offers fellows a scholarship. The work has somewhere to go from the first week.

EligibilityWho should apply

Atlas is built for students in grades 9 through 12, or the international equivalent, anywhere in the world. No prior research experience is required. That is the point. Interest in education, social science, policy, or global affairs matters more than a résumé.

DifferenceHow Atlas differs

Most research programs for high school students cost thousands of dollars, run in a single country, and produce work no one reads. Atlas is free, works across borders, and gives student work a path to publication from the start. Where a student lives should not decide whether they get to do research.
